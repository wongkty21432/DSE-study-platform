Corrosion, Rusting & Protection

▼

Rusting Requirements & Mechanism

Requires BOTH O₂ + H₂O — remove either, rusting stops.

Anode: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation)
Cathode: O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻ (reduction)
Overall: 2Fe + O₂ + 2H₂O → 2Fe(OH)₂ →(further oxidation) Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (RUST)

⚠️ Rust is flaky and porous — unlike Al₂O₃ on Al, it does NOT protect. Iron underneath continues to rust. Salt accelerates rusting (increases water conductivity).

Protection Methods

MethodHowExamples
BarrierBlock O₂+H₂OPaint, oil, Sn/Cr plating, plastic
Sacrificial (galvanizing)More reactive metal corrodes insteadZn coating on Fe, Mg anodes on ships
Cathodic (impressed current)DC makes Fe the cathodePipelines, bridges
AlloyingCr forms passive Cr₂O₃ layerStainless steel (Fe+Cr+Ni)
AnodizationThicken natural Al₂O₃ layerAluminium (anode in dil. H₂SO₄)
💡 Tin can scratch problem: Sn is LESS reactive than Fe → at scratch, Fe rusts FASTER (galvanic action). Zn galvanizing: Zn is MORE reactive → Zn corrodes instead, even at scratches.

Alkalis, Ammonia & Amphoteric Hydroxides

▼

Base vs. Alkali

Base: Reacts with acid → salt + water  |  Alkali: SOLUBLE base (produces OH⁻ in water). All alkalis are bases; NOT all bases are alkalis.

Ammonia Chemistry

NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ (weak alkali, ~1% ionized)
Test: Damp red litmus → BLUE | White fumes (NH₄Cl) with conc. HCl
Dry NH₃ preparation: NH₄Cl + NaOH →(heat) NH₃ + NaCl + H₂O
Drying: CaO (NOT conc. H₂SO₄ — reacts! NOT CaCl₂ — forms complex!)

Amphoteric Hydroxides

Dissolve in EXCESS NaOH: Zn(OH)₂ (white→colourless), Al(OH)₃ (white→colourless), Pb(OH)₂
Do NOT dissolve: Cu(OH)₂, Fe(OH)₂, Fe(OH)₃, Ca(OH)₂, Mg(OH)₂

💡 Classic exam question: "White ppt forms with few drops NaOH, dissolves in excess" → Zn²⁺ or Al³⁺

Molecular Polarity = Net Dipole?

NON-POLAR (symmetrical — dipoles cancel): CO₂, CCl₄, BF₃, CH₄

Intermolecular Forces (increasing strength)

H₂O vs. H₂S — Why H₂O bp is 189°C higher

Redox Fundamentals & Electrochemical Cells

▼
🧠 OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

Oxidation Number Rules

  1. Element = 0
  2. Simple ion = charge
  3. O = -2 (except peroxides -1)
  4. H = +1 (except metal hydrides -1)
  5. Sum = 0 for compound; = charge for ion

Electrochemical Series

K(-2.93) > Ca(-2.87) > Na(-2.71) > Mg(-2.37) > Al(-1.66) > Zn(-0.76) > Fe(-0.44) > H(0.00) > Cu(+0.34) > Ag(+0.80)

More negative E° = STRONGER reducing agent (more easily oxidized)

Zn-Cu Cell (Daniell Cell)

Anode (-): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (Zn dissolves)
Cathode (+): Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (Cu deposits)
E°cell = 1.10 V · Salt bridge allows ion flow, completes circuit

🔥

Enthalpy & Hess's Law

▼

Key Definitions

TermDefinition
ExothermicΔH < 0 — releases heat; products LOWER energy
EndothermicΔH > 0 — absorbs heat; products HIGHER energy
ΔH°fFormation of 1 mol compound from elements (elements = 0)
ΔH°cComplete combustion of 1 mol substance
ΔH°neutFormation of 1 mol H₂O from acid+alkali neutralization

Hess's Law

ΔH is PATH INDEPENDENT — depends only on initial and final states.

From formation: ΔH = ΣΔHf(products) - ΣΔHf(reactants)
From combustion: ΔH = ΣΔHc(reactants) - ΣΔHc(products)

Bond Energy Calculations

ΔH = Σ(bonds BROKEN) - Σ(bonds FORMED)
Breaking = ENDOTHERMIC (+) · Forming = EXOTHERMIC (-)

Calorimetry

ΔH = -m × c × ΔT where m=mass(g), c=4.18 J/g°C, ΔT=temp change

Collision Theory & Factors Affecting Rate

▼

Three Requirements for Reaction

Particles must: (1) COLLIDE · (2) Correct ORIENTATION · (3) Energy ≥ Ea

Five Factors

FactorEffectMechanism
Concentration ↑FasterMore particles → more collisions
Pressure ↑ (gases)FasterSame as concentration
Temperature ↑MAJOR ↑More collisions + more exceed Ea. ~doubles per 10°C
Surface area ↑FasterMore exposed particles (powder > lumps)
CatalystFasterAlternative pathway, LOWER Ea. Not consumed.
⚠️ Catalyst does NOT change ΔH or equilibrium position — only speed of reaching equilibrium.

Molar Volume

RTP (25°C, 1 atm): 24.0 dm³/mol  |  STP (0°C, 1 atm): 22.4 dm³/mol

Gas Collection Methods

Upward delivery (lighter than air: NH₃, H₂) · Downward delivery (heavier: Cl₂, CO₂, SO₂) · Over water (insoluble: H₂, O₂, N₂) · Gas syringe (any gas)

Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle

▼

Dynamic Equilibrium

Forward rate = backward rate · Concentrations constant · Closed system required

Le Chatelier's Principle — Summary

ChangeShiftKc change?
Add reactant→ RIGHTNO
Remove product→ RIGHTNO
Increase P (fewer gas mol on right)→ RIGHTNO
Increase T (exothermic ΔH<0)← LEFTYES (decreases)
Increase T (endothermic ΔH>0)→ RIGHTYES (increases)
Add catalystNO shiftNO

Kc Expression

For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ · Only T changes Kc!

Haber Process: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃   ΔH = -92 kJ/mol

450°C · 200 atm · Fe catalyst · Recycling → ~98% overall yield

Contact Process: 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃   ΔH = -197 kJ/mol

450°C · 2 atm · V₂O₅ catalyst

⚠️ Common mistakes: Catalyst ≠ more yield · Equal rates ≠ equal concentrations · Kc only changes with T · Adding solid ≠ shift equilibrium
